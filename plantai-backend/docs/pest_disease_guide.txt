Common Pests and Diseases for Pennsylvania Vegetable Crops: Tomatoes, Corn, Peppers, Beans, and Squash

Pennsylvania's humid continental climate creates favorable conditions for a wide range of agricultural pests and diseases. Effective integrated pest management (IPM) begins with identification, economic thresholds, and cultural controls before resorting to pesticide applications.

TOMATOES

Pests:
Tomato hornworm (Manduca quinquemaculata): Large green caterpillars (3–4 inches) with white diagonal stripes and a rear horn. Feed voraciously on foliage and fruit. Hand-pick when populations are low. Parasitic wasps (Cotesia congregatus) are effective biological control — do not kill hornworms with white egg sacs attached, as parasitic wasps have already attacked them. Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis) is effective when caterpillars are small.
Aphids (multiple species): Cluster on undersides of leaves and new growth. Vector viruses including tomato mosaic virus. Knock off with water stream. Introduce ladybugs or lacewings. Insecticidal soap (1–2% solution) effective for heavy infestations.
Tomato fruitworm (Helicoverpa zea): Enters fruit at stem end. Destroy infested fruit. Spinosad or Bt applied when moths are active.

Diseases:
Late blight (Phytophthora infestans): Most destructive tomato disease in Pennsylvania. Water-soaked lesions on leaves, white powdery growth on undersides in high humidity, rapid collapse of vines. Favored by cool nights (50–65°F), warm humid days, and leaf wetness. Plant resistant varieties (Mountain Merit, Defiant PHR, Jasper). Apply copper fungicide or chlorothalonil preventatively. Scout weekly starting July 1.
Early blight (Alternaria solani): Brown target-spot lesions on lower leaves; progresses upward. Maintain good air circulation, avoid overhead irrigation, remove infected foliage. Apply mancozeb or chlorothalonil at 7–10 day intervals under wet conditions.
Fusarium wilt: Yellowing and wilting of one side of plant, brown vascular tissue when stem is cut. No cure — remove and destroy affected plants. Plant resistant varieties (labeled "F" on seed packets). Crop rotate 3–4 years.

SWEET CORN

Pests:
Corn earworm (Helicoverpa zea): Primary pest of sweet corn. Larvae enter silks and feed on developing kernels at ear tip. Monitor with pheromone traps; apply Bt or spinosad to silks every 3–5 days when trap captures exceed 5 moths/night during silking. Mineral oil (1 mL) applied to silk 3–5 days after silk emergence disrupts larval access.
European corn borer (Ostrinia nubilalis): Two generations in Pennsylvania. First-generation larvae bore into stalks (June–July), second into ears (August). Pheromone trap monitoring critical. Bt applied at egg hatch (first instar) is highly effective.
Japanese beetle (Popillia japonica): Adults feed on corn silks, reducing pollination and kernel set. Hand-pick adults from silks during morning hours when sluggish.

Diseases:
Northern corn leaf blight (Exserohilum turcicum): Long, cigar-shaped gray-green lesions on leaves. Plant resistant hybrids. Fungicide (azoxystrobin or pyraclostrobin) application at VT/R1 stage economically justified when disease present before tasseling.
Common smut (Ustilago maydis): Gray-silver galls on ears, tassels, and stalks that rupture to release black spores. No effective chemical control. Remove and destroy galls before they rupture. Plant resistant hybrids.

BELL PEPPERS

Pests:
Pepper weevil (Anthonomus eugenii): Adults lay eggs in flower buds and small fruit; larvae destroy seeds and cause fruit drop. Monitor with yellow sticky traps. Spinosad or permethrin when threshold reached.
European corn borer: Also infests pepper stems in second generation. Same management as in corn.
Aphids: As with tomatoes — insecticidal soap and biological controls.

Diseases:
Phytophthora blight (Phytophthora capsici): Crown and root rot, foliar blight, fruit rot. Most serious pepper disease in Pennsylvania's wet summers. Avoid planting in poorly drained soils. Raised beds critical. Rotate to non-susceptible crops for 3+ years. Apply mefenoxam (Ridomil) preventatively in high-risk situations.
Bacterial leaf spot (Xanthomonas campestris): Water-soaked lesions turning brown with yellow halos. Avoid overhead irrigation. Copper bactericide at 7–10 day intervals during wet weather.

GREEN BEANS

Pests:
Mexican bean beetle (Epilachna varivestis): Copper-colored beetle resembling ladybug; spiny yellow larvae skeletonize leaf undersides. Early plantings often escape worst damage. Neem oil or pyrethrin when larvae present.
Bean leaf beetle: Feeds on leaves and pods. Plant early to avoid peak populations. Pyrethrin or spinosad for heavy infestations.

Diseases:
White mold (Sclerotinia sclerotiorum): Cottony white fungal growth on stems and pods in cool, wet conditions. Improve air circulation through plant spacing. Fungicide (iprodione or thiophanate-methyl) at early bloom.
Bean common mosaic virus: Spread by aphids. Plant resistant varieties. Control aphids to reduce spread.

SQUASH AND ZUCCHINI

Pests:
Squash vine borer (Melittia cucurbitae): Larvae bore into base of stems; plants wilt suddenly. Wrap base of stems in aluminum foil to deter egg laying. Apply Bt or spinosad to base of plant weekly when adult moths (clear-winged, red and black) are active (late June–July).
Cucumber beetles (striped and spotted): Feed on foliage and spread bacterial wilt. Row covers from transplant until flowering provide protection. Remove covers at flowering for pollination. Kaolin clay as deterrent. Pyrethrin if threshold exceeded.
Squash bug (Anasa tristis): Gray-brown bugs cluster on undersides of leaves. Egg masses orange-bronze, in neat rows on leaf undersides. Hand-pick eggs. Neem oil or pyrethrin on nymphs. Remove plant debris after harvest.

Diseases:
Powdery mildew (Podosphaera xanthii): White powdery coating on leaves. Very common in Pennsylvania by late July. Reduce nitrogen, improve air circulation. Resistant varieties best. Potassium bicarbonate or sulfur fungicide effective early.
Downy mildew: Angular yellow lesions on upper leaf surface, purplish sporulation below. Favored by cool nights and leaf wetness. Apply copper or chlorothalonil preventatively.

General IPM Principles for Pennsylvania Vegetable Farms

Scout crops weekly. Use economic thresholds — not all pest presence requires treatment. Prioritize biological controls, cultural practices (rotation, sanitation, resistant varieties), and physical barriers. Reserve synthetic pesticides for threshold-exceeding situations. Maintain records of all scouting observations and pesticide applications as required for most USDA conservation program compliance.
