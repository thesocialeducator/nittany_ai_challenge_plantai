USDA Conservation Stewardship Program (CSP) — Overview, Payment Rates, and Eligible Practices

The Conservation Stewardship Program (CSP) is USDA's largest conservation program by acreage and is designed to reward farmers who are already implementing strong conservation practices while encouraging them to do more. Unlike EQIP, which is project-based, CSP provides annual stewardship payments for maintaining and improving conservation systems across an entire farm operation over a 5-year renewable contract period.

Program Overview and Philosophy

CSP operates on the principle of "payment for performance." Farmers are paid annually based on their stewardship performance index score, which measures how many conservation activities they implement and how well those activities address resource concerns on their land. The program covers the full operation, not individual practices, and rewards comprehensive, integrated conservation approaches. CSP is administered by USDA NRCS and funded at approximately $700 million per year nationally.

Who Is Eligible for CSP?

Eligibility requirements are similar to EQIP: farmers must operate eligible agricultural land, comply with highly erodible land and wetland provisions, and have an adjusted gross income under $900,000. Unlike EQIP, CSP requires that applicants already meet a minimum threshold of conservation activities before applying. Priority is given to: beginning farmers (farming less than 10 years), socially disadvantaged farmers, and operations transitioning to organic.

Payment Structure and Rates

CSP payments are calculated annually based on the stewardship performance index score (0–100 scale). Payments include:

Base payment: $18–$22/acre for cropland in Pennsylvania, based on meeting existing stewardship activities threshold.
Enhancement payments: Additional $5–$45/acre for implementing new high-priority enhancements such as:
- Organic transition or certification: up to $45/acre additional
- Cover crop species diversification: $8–$18/acre
- Integrated pest management improvements: $5–$12/acre
- Precision nutrient application: $6–$15/acre
- Pollinator and beneficial insect habitat: $15–$30/acre established
- Soil health assessment and testing beyond minimum: $3–$8/acre
- Reduced tillage or no-till systems: $10–$20/acre

Maximum annual CSP payment is $40,000/year, or $200,000 over the 5-year contract period. Operations with multiple qualifying enterprise types (crop + livestock) may reach higher totals. After 5 years, contracts can be renewed if the operation continues to meet conservation goals.

Key Eligible Enhancements for Pennsylvania Vegetable Farms

For small vegetable farms in Pennsylvania, the highest-value CSP enhancements typically include:

1. Cover crop management: Implementing diverse cover crop mixes (3+ species) with termination timing to optimize soil health. Documentation via field records required.
2. Nutrient management: Precision testing every 2 years, variable-rate nutrient application, and keeping detailed nutrient application records.
3. Integrated pest management: Scouting records, economic thresholds, preference for biological and cultural controls over chemical controls.
4. Pollinator habitat: Establishing 1–3% of farm acreage in native wildflower strips or border plantings. Preferred plant lists available from Penn State Extension.
5. Water quality: Installing vegetative buffer strips (35+ feet) along streams, ponds, or drainage ditches.

Application Process

1. Contact local NRCS office for a farm assessment and stewardship performance index ranking.
2. Applications accepted on a continuous basis; ranked and funded in 2 cycles per year.
3. Develop a CSP stewardship plan with NRCS identifying current practices, proposed enhancements, and payment estimates.
4. Sign 5-year contract. Annual payments made after NRCS verification of practice implementation.
5. Annual self-certification reports required documenting implemented practices.

Comparison: CSP vs. EQIP

EQIP: Best for implementing specific capital-intensive new practices (irrigation systems, high tunnels, drainage). One-time or project-based payments. EQIP does not require existing conservation baseline.
CSP: Best for farms already engaged in conservation that want annual income support for maintaining and improving whole-farm conservation. Requires existing conservation baseline. Provides steady annual income over 5 years.

Many farmers participate in both programs simultaneously — EQIP for capital projects, CSP for whole-farm stewardship payments.
