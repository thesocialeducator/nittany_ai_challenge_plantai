Penn State Extension — Zone 6b Planting Calendar for Central Pennsylvania

USDA Hardiness Zone 6b encompasses most of central Pennsylvania including Centre County (State College), Blair County, and surrounding areas. Average annual extreme minimum temperatures range from -5°F to 0°F (-20.6°C to -17.8°C). The average last spring frost date is April 15–April 30, and the first fall frost typically arrives October 1–October 15, yielding a growing season of approximately 155–175 days.

JANUARY – FEBRUARY: Indoor Seed Starting
Start onions and leeks indoors 10–12 weeks before last frost (early to mid-February). Begin celeriac and celery indoors in late February. Use grow lights for 14–16 hours per day. Maintain soil temperature of 70–75°F for germination. Review and order seeds; prioritize disease-resistant varieties for Pennsylvania conditions.

MARCH: Early Season Preparations
March 1–15: Direct sow cold-hardy crops into a cold frame or low tunnel — spinach, mâche, arugula, claytonia. Soil temperature must reach 40°F for germination. Start peppers indoors March 1–15 (8–10 weeks before transplant date). Start tomatoes indoors March 15–31. Harden off transplants by gradually exposing to outdoor temperatures starting 1 week before planting. Apply lime if soil test shows pH below 6.0.

APRIL: Spring Planting Begins
April 1–15: Direct sow peas, spinach, lettuce, kale, chard, beets, carrots, radishes, and turnips outdoors. Soil temperature should be 45–50°F minimum. Transplant onion starts and leek starts. Apply pre-emergent weed control or lay down plastic mulch for warm-season crops.
April 15–30 (last frost zone): Transplant broccoli, cabbage, cauliflower, and Brussels sprouts started indoors. These tolerate light frost. Begin preparing beds for warm-season crops.

MAY: Warm-Season Planting
May 1–15: Direct sow beets, carrots, chard, beans (after soil warms to 60°F). Plant potatoes when soil temperature reaches 50°F (typically May 1–15 in Zone 6b). Set out hardened tomato transplants around May 15 — after all danger of frost has passed.
May 15–31: Transplant peppers, eggplant, and basil after nighttime temperatures consistently stay above 55°F. Direct sow summer squash, zucchini, cucumbers, and winter squash. Plant corn when soil temperature is 60°F+ (typically May 15–31).

JUNE – JULY: Summer Growing
Succession-plant lettuce, beans, and radishes every 2–3 weeks for continuous harvest. Direct sow second planting of sweet corn June 1–15 for succession harvest. Monitor pest pressure closely in June — Colorado potato beetle, cabbage worms, and aphids peak in early summer. Side-dress heavy feeders (corn, tomatoes, peppers) with nitrogen around June 15.

AUGUST: Fall Crop Establishment
August 1–15: Direct sow fall brassicas — broccoli, cabbage, kale, turnips — for October harvest. Start spinach, lettuce, and arugula for fall harvest. Plant garlic cloves in late August for overwintering. Begin reducing irrigation for crops intended for storage (onions, potatoes, winter squash).

SEPTEMBER – OCTOBER: Fall Harvest and Cover Crops
Harvest summer crops before first frost (October 1–15). Cure potatoes, winter squash, and sweet potatoes at 60–70°F for 2 weeks before storage. Direct sow spinach and mâche under row covers for overwintering. Plant winter rye, crimson clover, or hairy vetch as cover crops September 1–October 1 for best establishment before freeze.

OCTOBER – NOVEMBER: Season Close
Harvest root crops after light frost — flavor improves with cold exposure. Plant garlic October 1–31, 2 inches deep, 6 inches apart. Mulch garlic beds with straw after ground freezes. Apply compost or manure to all beds. Till under or mow cover crops in fall if spring termination timing is a concern.

Key Temperature Thresholds for Zone 6b Pennsylvania
- Frost-tolerant crops (plant April 1): spinach, kale, peas, lettuce, brassicas
- Frost-sensitive crops (plant May 15): tomatoes, peppers, basil, cucumbers, squash, beans
- Heat-loving crops needing 60°F soil: corn, melons, sweet potatoes
